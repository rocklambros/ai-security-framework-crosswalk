Table~\ref{tab:main_results} summarizes performance on the 400
expert-labeled frozen test set. The full 22-feature model achieves:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Tier accuracy:} 0.5325 (95\% CI [0.485, 0.580])
    \item \textbf{Macro F1:} 0.4888 (95\% CI [0.433, 0.538])
    \item \textbf{Adjacent accuracy:} 0.8425 --- 84\% of predictions
          fall within one tier of the ground-truth label
    \item \textbf{Binary accuracy:} 0.7125
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Per-Class Analysis}

Performance varies substantially across tiers. The Related class, which
dominates the test distribution (186/400), achieves the highest F1
(0.613) and accuracy (0.640). The Unrelated class (78/400) benefits from
clear semantic separation, reaching F1 of 0.525 and accuracy of 0.615.

The Equivalent class (58/400) achieves F1 of 0.454 and accuracy of
0.379. While below the overall average, this represents meaningful
discrimination for the most demanding tier, where the model must
distinguish full semantic equivalence from strong relatedness. The
Partial class (78/400) remains the most challenging at F1 of 0.364 and
accuracy of 0.308, consistent with the inherent subjectivity of
partial-overlap judgments noted in prior crosswalk studies.

The confusion matrix reveals that the primary error mode is
over-prediction of the Related tier: 28/58 Equivalent pairs are
predicted as Related, and 35/78 Partial pairs are predicted as Related.
This ``gravitational pull'' toward the majority class persists despite
inverse-frequency weighting, suggesting that the boundary between
Related and its neighboring tiers is genuinely ambiguous in the feature
space.

\subsection{Conformal Coverage}

The Mondrian conformal predictor achieves empirical coverage of 93.8\%
at $\alpha = 0.10$, closely meeting the nominal 90\% target. The mean
prediction set size is 3.20 out of four possible classes, indicating
that the conformal wrapper is conservative: for most instances, it
retains three of the four tiers in the prediction set to maintain
coverage. This conservatism is expected given the ordinal structure of
the tier scheme and the moderate discriminative power of the underlying
classifier. Figure~\ref{fig:conformal_sets} shows the distribution of
prediction set sizes stratified by true tier.

\subsection{Expert--LLM Label Gap}

On the 379 LLM-labeled validation set, accuracy rises to approximately
0.65, reflecting a distribution gap between expert and LLM labeling.
This gap is most pronounced for the Partial and Equivalent tiers, where
expert annotators apply stricter criteria for semantic overlap than the
LLM-SME labeling protocol. The gap motivates our emphasis on
expert-labeled evaluation and highlights the importance of the frozen
test set for honest benchmarking.
